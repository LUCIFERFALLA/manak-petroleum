Multipurpose grease for cars — types, uses & buying guide.

"Multipurpose grease" sounds like one thing — but in a car, you're really lubricating ten different jobs with very different demands. Here's how to pick a grease that handles most of them, and where you actually need something specialised.

What grease really does in your car

Engine oil bathes hot, fast-moving parts. Grease is for slow, loaded, water- and dust-exposed surfaces where an oil would just drip away. A car has dozens of these points: wheel bearings, U-joints, suspension ball joints, tie-rod ends, brake-caliper slides, door hinges, hood latches, parking-brake cables, sometimes even the gear lever pivot.

NLGI grades — what's #2 and why you want it

NLGI (National Lubricating Grease Institute) classifies greases by consistency, from 000 (almost-liquid) to 6 (block-hard). For cars, NLGI 2 is the universal standard — peanut-butter consistency, stiff enough to stay in bearings, soft enough to pump through a grease gun.

  • NLGI 1 — softer, easier pumping, used in central-lubrication systems
  • NLGI 2 — the default for automotive bearings and chassis
  • NLGI 3 — stiffer, for very-high-speed bearings and sealed-for-life applications

Lithium vs lithium complex vs calcium sulfonate

The thickener (the "soap" that holds the oil) is what makes one grease different from another. The three you'll see in automotive:

  • Lithium soap — the original multipurpose grease. Good general-purpose, water-resistant, drop point ~190°C. Cheap and capable.
  • Lithium complex — upgraded lithium. Drop point 260°C+, better high-temperature stability. The best all-rounder for modern cars with disc brakes.
  • Calcium sulfonate complex — top-tier. Drop point above 300°C, exceptional water resistance, EP-loaded. Use it for severe-service trucks, marine, or as a premium chassis grease.

For a deeper comparison, see our guide on lithium vs calcium vs lithium-complex grease.

Don't mix incompatible greases

This trips up workshops every week. Lithium + polyurea = bad. Lithium + calcium sulfonate = bad. The thickeners react, the structure breaks down, and what was solid grease becomes runny oil that drips out. If you change grease type, clean out the old grease completely first.

Rule of thumb: pick one good NLGI 2 lithium-complex grease and use it everywhere multipurpose grease is called for. Consistency beats variety.

How to read a grease label

A good automotive grease label will tell you all four of these:

  1. Thickener type — lithium / lithium complex / calcium sulfonate / polyurea / etc.
  2. NLGI grade — 1, 2, or 3
  3. Base oil viscosity — usually ISO VG 150 or 220 for automotive
  4. Drop point — the temperature at which the grease melts. For modern cars: 230°C+ minimum, 260°C+ preferred.

Frequently asked questions

What is multipurpose grease used for in cars?

Wheel bearings, chassis lubrication points, U-joints, suspension ball joints, door hinges, tie-rod ends, and brake-caliper slides. A good NLGI 2 lithium or lithium-complex grease covers most of these applications.

Which grease is best for car wheel bearings?

A high-temperature NLGI 2 lithium-complex grease with a drop point above 250°C. For disc-brake hubs that run hot, calcium sulfonate complex grease is even better.

What is NLGI 2 grease?

NLGI 2 is a consistency rating — peanut-butter texture, the standard for automotive bearings and chassis. Stiff enough to stay in place, soft enough to pump through a grease gun.

Can I mix different types of grease?

No. Mixing incompatible thickeners (lithium + calcium sulfonate, lithium + polyurea) breaks the structure down. If you switch types, clean the old grease out first.

How often should car grease be replaced?

Most sealed components are lifetime-greased. Open chassis points need re-greasing every 5,000–10,000 km. Wheel bearings on traditional designs should be repacked per the service manual.
